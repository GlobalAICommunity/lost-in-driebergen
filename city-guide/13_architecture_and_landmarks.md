Architecture & Landmarks

Driebergen-Rijsenburg's architectural character is defined by its estate heritage: country houses, gate lodges, coach houses, and the mature parkland that frames them.

## The Estates

The village has an unusual concentration of historical estates, most dating from the seventeenth to nineteenth centuries:

**Sterkenburg** on the Sterkenburgerlaan is a medieval-origin castle rebuilt in neo-Gothic style in the nineteenth century. Its tower is visible above the trees from several points in the village. Private residence, not open to the public, but the surrounding lanes offer good views.

**Sparrendaal** on the Arnhemse Bovenweg is a large estate with a nineteenth-century main house set in extensive parkland with exotic trees. The grounds are accessible for walking.

**Dennenburg** on the Hoofdstraat is a white neoclassical villa behind iron gates, now used as offices. Its symmetrical facade is one of the most photographed buildings in the village.

**Bornia** is an estate that gave its name to the surrounding forest. The house itself is private, but the forest paths that once formed its pleasure grounds are public walking territory.

## Churches

**Grote Kerk (Reformed Church)** on the Hoofdstraat dates from the fifteenth century, with a distinctive tower visible from across the village. The interior was stripped during the Reformation but retains medieval proportions.

**Sint Petrus' Banden** in Rijsenburg is the Catholic parish church, built in 1876 in neo-Gothic style. Its presence reflects Rijsenburg's historically Catholic identity in an otherwise Protestant region.

## Driebergen-Zeist Station

The railway station, built in 1901, is a Rijksmonument (national heritage building). Its Art Nouveau / Jugendstil-influenced design with decorative ironwork and a distinctive canopy makes it one of the more architecturally notable small stations in the Netherlands.

## Villa Architecture

The streets between the station and village center — particularly the Bosstraat, Welgelegenlaan, and Sparrenlaan — are lined with late-nineteenth and early-twentieth-century villas in styles ranging from Swiss chalet to Amsterdam School to restrained Art Deco. A walk through these streets is an informal architecture tour.

## Pyramide van Austerlitz

Built in 1804 by Napoleonic troops as a monument to their general Auguste de Marmont, this 36-meter earthen pyramid topped with a stone obelisk is the most unusual landmark on the Heuvelrug. Restored in 2008, it is now a recognized heritage site and the symbol of the Austerlitz village.
