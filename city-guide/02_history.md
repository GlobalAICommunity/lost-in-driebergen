A Brief History

The name Driebergen means "three hills," referring to the sandy ridges that mark the landscape. The village first appears in records from the thirteenth century, when the Bishop of Utrecht granted land rights in the area. For most of its early history, Driebergen was a small farming settlement on poor sandy soil, overshadowed by the nearby town of Zeist and the fortress at Wijk bij Duurstede.

## The Estate Era

Everything changed in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Wealthy merchants from Utrecht and Amsterdam discovered the ridge's appeal: elevated ground, clean air, and forests that could be sculpted into formal gardens. They built country estates along the Hoofdstraat and the wooded lanes branching off it. Sparrendaal, Sterkenburg, Dennenburg, and Bornia all date from this period. These estates defined the character of the village and many survive today, either as private residences, institutions, or protected monuments.

## Rijsenburg

Rijsenburg, the smaller neighbor to the south, has its own distinct identity. It grew around a Catholic community that maintained its faith during the Protestant Republic, worshipping in a schuilkerk (hidden church). The two villages merged administratively in 1857 and have been known jointly as Driebergen-Rijsenburg ever since, though locals still distinguish between them.

## The Railway

The arrival of the railway in 1845 connected Driebergen to Utrecht and Arnhem, making it practical for the wealthy to live on the ridge year-round rather than only in summer. The station — now called Driebergen-Zeist — brought a building boom of villas and pensions in the late nineteenth century. The village grew from a summer retreat into a permanent residential community.

## The Twentieth Century

During the Second World War, the Heuvelrug was heavily forested and relatively remote, which made it both a hiding place for people in the resistance and a target for German occupation forces who took over several estates. After the war, many of the large estates were converted into conference centers, care homes, and educational institutions — a pattern that continues today.

## Municipal Merger

In 2006, Driebergen-Rijsenburg merged with the neighboring municipalities of Doorn, Leersum, Amerongen, and Maarn to form the municipality of Utrechtse Heuvelrug. The new municipality stretches across the full length of the ridge, from Zeist in the northwest to the Rhine floodplain near Rhenen in the southeast. Driebergen-Rijsenburg remains the most populated village in the municipality, with roughly 12,000 inhabitants.

## Today

Modern Driebergen-Rijsenburg balances its estate heritage with village life: a compact shopping street, a weekly market, a train station with direct connections to Utrecht and Arnhem, and immediate access to thousands of hectares of protected forest. It is a place that has found its identity in the space between city and countryside.
