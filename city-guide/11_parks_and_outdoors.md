Parks & the Outdoors

The forests surrounding Driebergen are the defining feature of life on the Heuvelrug. Unlike many Dutch villages surrounded by flat polder, Driebergen sits in a landscape of rolling hills, beech forests, heathland, and estate parks. You can walk from the village center into deep forest in under five minutes.

## Heidestein and Bornia

Directly north of the village, the Heidestein-Bornia nature reserve is the closest large natural area. Managed by Utrechts Landschap, it combines dense deciduous forest with patches of open heathland (heide). In August and September, the heather blooms purple across the open areas. Walking and cycling paths are well-maintained, and the terrain has gentle hills — unusual for the Netherlands.

## Sparrendaal

East of the village along the Arnhemse Bovenweg, the Sparrendaal estate grounds are open to walkers. The mature trees — copper beeches, oaks, and exotic conifers planted by the original estate owners — create a cathedral-like atmosphere. Sparrendaal is a particularly beautiful walk in autumn when the canopy turns gold and copper.

## Ridderoordse Bos

South of Rijsenburg, this forest connects to the open landscape of the Langbroekerwetering. It is quieter than Heidestein, with fewer cyclists and more birdlife. The transition from forest to polder is abrupt and striking — dense trees give way suddenly to wide views over flat agricultural land with willows lining drainage ditches.

## The Pyramide van Austerlitz

A 30-minute walk or 10-minute cycle east through the forest brings you to the Pyramide van Austerlitz, a 36-meter earthen pyramid built by French soldiers in 1804 during the Napoleonic occupation. Climb to the top for views over the surrounding forest canopy. The area around the pyramid includes walking trails, a visitors center, and a café.

## Darthuizerpoort (Leersum)

Further southeast, the Darthuizerpoort is a dramatic notch cut through the ridge by meltwater during the last ice age. The viewpoint looks out over the Rhine valley toward the Betuwe. The walk from the car park to the viewpoint is short (500 meters) but the landscape feels alpine by Dutch standards.

## Cycling the Heuvelrug

The ridge itself is a continuous green corridor from Zeist to Rhenen, and it is possible to cycle the full length (approximately 35 km) almost entirely through forest. The fietsknooppunten network makes navigation simple: follow numbered junctions and the signs do the rest.

## Wildlife

The Heuvelrug forests support roe deer, foxes, badgers, numerous woodpecker species (great spotted, green, and black), and — if you are patient and quiet at dusk — wild boar in the eastern sections near Amerongen.
